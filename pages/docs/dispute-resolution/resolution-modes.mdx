# Resolution Modes: v1, v2, v3

Sew's dispute resolution has three versions, each adding complexity and decentralization as the protocol matures.

## Version 1: Single Trusted Resolver

**Status**: 🟢 Active at launch

### Design

One governance-controlled resolver makes binding decisions for all disputes. **Alternatively, a custom resolver can be specified per-escrow** by passing a `customResolver` address at creation, allowing applications to designate their own resolver.

```
User Opens Dispute
        ↓
    Resolver Reviews
    (governance-default OR custom-specified)
        ↓
   Decision Final
        ↓
  Funds Distributed
```

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governance** | Controlled by protocol governance |
| **Decision Path** | One-level: direct to resolver |
| **Appeal Options** | None; governance can override |
| **Speed** | Fast (hours to days) |
| **Decentralization** | Low (single point of trust) |
| **Capital Required** | None from resolver (trusted entity) |
| **Appeal Bonds** | Not applicable |

### Pros

- ✅ **Simple**: Minimal complexity, easy to understand
- ✅ **Fast**: No escalation delays, decisions are swift
- ✅ **Governance control**: Protocol can adjust resolver if needed
- ✅ **Low overhead**: No bond requirements, minimal infrastructure

### Cons

- ❌ **Single point of failure**: If resolver is compromised, all disputes are at risk
- ❌ **Limited expertise**: One resolver may not have category-specific knowledge
- ❌ **Scaling bottleneck**: Can become slow if disputes are high volume

### Use Cases

- Bootstrap phase when dispute volume is low
- High-trust environments with established governance
- Simple dispute categories requiring quick turnaround

### Parameters (v1)

- **Resolver**: Governance-appointed address
- **Custom Resolver**: Can be customized by passing a `customResolver` address at escrow creation (allows per-escrow resolver configuration)
- **Resolution deadline**: 24-48 hours from dispute opening
- **No appeal mechanism**: Governance override only

## Version 2: Decentralized Resolution

**Status**: 🟡 Built, deferred until post-v1 (governance activation required)

### Design

Multiple resolvers with staking and appeal bonds. Decisions can be escalated with economic penalties for frivolous appeals.

```
User Opens Dispute
        ↓
  Resolver Level 1 Reviews
  (posting bond requirement)
        ↓
  Decision Made
        ↓
   Party Disagrees?
        ↓
   Post Appeal Bond
        ↓
  Resolver Level 2 (Senior) Reviews
        ↓
   Final Decision
        ↓
  Funds Distributed
```

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governance** | Governance selects resolvers; v2 protocols define incentives |
| **Decision Paths** | Two-level: standard → senior |
| **Appeal Options** | One escalation level with bond |
| **Speed** | Medium (1-3 days typical) |
| **Decentralization** | Medium (multiple resolvers, governance-selected) |
| **Capital Required** | Resolver staking (minimum bond) |
| **Appeal Bonds** | Required to escalate; forfeited if appeal fails |

### Key Mechanisms

#### Resolver Staking

Resolvers post bonds to participate:
- Minimum bond required per resolver tier
- Bonds serve as collateral for slashing
- Frozen if insufficient bond remains

#### Appeal Bonds

Appellants post collateral to escalate:
- Amount: Typically 5-10% of dispute value
- Outcome: Forfeited if appeal fails; returned if it succeeds
- Effect: Discourages frivolous escalation

#### Slashing

Automatic penalties for resolver misconduct:
- **Timeout**: Missed deadline → 2% of bond slashed
- **Reversal**: Decision overturned → 5% of bond slashed
- **Severe**: Multiple reversals in epoch → 10% slashed + freeze

Slashing protects the system by making dishonest resolution costly.

### Pros

- ✅ **Economic incentives**: Slashing and bonds align behavior
- ✅ **Scalability**: Multiple resolvers handle more disputes
- ✅ **Frivolous appeal prevention**: Bond cost discourages weak escalations
- ✅ **Specialist selection**: Can assign category experts
- ✅ **Transparency**: All decisions recorded on-chain

### Cons

- ❌ **Complexity**: Bond mechanics add operational overhead
- ❌ **Still governance-dependent**: Resolver selection is governance-voted
- ❌ **Resolver centralization risk**: A few resolvers may dominate
- ❌ **Capital barriers**: Bond requirements may limit operator entry

### Use Cases

- Medium-scale deployment with significant dispute volume
- Disputes requiring specialist expertise (e.g., IP, contracts)
- Systems where multiple rounds of review are valuable

### Parameters (v2) - Conservative Launch Defaults

**Resolver Bonds:**
- Minimum: $250 per resolver
- Recommended: $500-$1,000 for reliable operator status

**Appeal Bonds:**
- Typical: 5% of dispute value
- Range: 2%-10% depending on dispute category

**Slashing:**
- Timeout: 2% of resolver bond
- Reversal: 5% of resolver bond
- Severe (freeze): 10% + 7-day operational freeze

**Timing:**
- Level 1 resolution deadline: 24 hours
- Level 2 (senior) deadline: 48 hours
- Appeal window: 48 hours after decision

## Version 3: Decentralized Incentives

**Status**: 🔵 Future (architecture designed, implementation staged)

### Design

Fully decentralized resolver selection with capital-weighted delegation. Seniors appoint and curate juniors; capital requirements scale with responsibility.

```
User Opens Dispute
        ↓
  Disputed Category Selected
        ↓
  Resolver Chosen (Capital-Weighted)
        ↓
  Level 1 Review (24h)
        ↓
   Disagree?
        ↓
  Senior Review (48h)
        ↓
   Disagree?
        ↓
  External Arbitration (Kleros, etc.)
        ↓
  Final Binding Outcome
        ↓
  Funds Distributed
```

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governance** | Governance sets parameters; resolver selection is fully decentralized |
| **Decision Paths** | Three-level: resolver → senior → external arbitration |
| **Appeal Options** | Two escalation levels with bonds |
| **Speed** | Slower (2-5 days typical) |
| **Decentralization** | High (decentralized resolver selection + capital-weighted) |
| **Capital Required** | Significant (3-10x dispute value reserves) |
| **Appeal Bonds** | Required at each level |

### Key Mechanisms

#### Capital-Weighted Delegation

Seniors delegate to juniors, but:
- Each appointment "reserves" coverage = junior_bond × multiplier (M=3)
- Senior can delegate up to: senior_bond × utilization_factor
- Forces alignment: bad juniors reduce senior's capacity

**Example:** Senior with $100k bond can appoint ~33 resolvers with $500 bonds (at M=3, U=0.5)

#### Resolver Staking (Advanced)

- **Resolver bond**: $250-$500 minimum (covers ~$1k-$2k disputes)
- **Senior bond**: $25,000-$100,000 minimum (insurance layer)
- **Delegation multiplier (M)**: 3x (conservative default)
- **Utilization factor (U)**: 0.5 (headroom for correlated penalties)

#### Multi-Level Slashing

Slashing waterfall:
1. **Junior resolver**: Takes first hit for timeouts/reversals
2. **Senior**: Backs junior's bond; takes hit for senior-level reversals
3. **Insurance pool**: Catastrophic losses spread across network

#### Epoch-Based Caps

To prevent cascading insolvency:
- Per-epoch slashing cap: e.g., 5% of total senior bond across protocol
- Rate-limited escalations: Max appeals per category per epoch
- Circuit breakers: Pause resolution if too many timeouts

### Pros

- ✅ **True decentralization**: No single resolver or governance bottleneck
- ✅ **Incentive alignment**: Capital at risk motivates honest decisions
- ✅ **Scalability**: Network of resolvers can handle arbitrary dispute volume
- ✅ **Specialization**: Category-based selection matches expertise with disputes
- ✅ **Insurance model**: Multi-layer capital protects all parties

### Cons

- ❌ **High complexity**: Delegation, slashing, and caps require careful tuning
- ❌ **Capital requirements**: Large bonds needed for participation
- ❌ **Slower decisions**: Three levels of escalation take time
- ❌ **Parameter risk**: Wrong settings can cause unintended freezes or insolvency

### Use Cases

- Mature protocol with significant dispute volume
- High-value transfers requiring multiple review levels
- Systems where network-wide security is paramount
- Long-term vision for fully decentralized dispute resolution

### Parameters (v3) - Conservative Launch Defaults

**Resolver Bonds:**
- Minimum: $250
- Recommended: $500 (reliable operator)
- Max escrow coverage: 4x resolver bond (capped at $2,000)

**Senior Bonds:**
- Minimum: $25,000
- Recommended: $50,000-$100,000 (mainnet confidence)

**Delegation:**
- Coverage multiplier (M): 3 (conservative)
- Utilization factor (U): 0.5 (headroom buffer)
- Max junior per senior: ~33 resolvers @ M=3, U=0.5

**Timing:**
- Level 1 resolution deadline: 24 hours
- Level 2 (senior) deadline: 48 hours
- Level 3 (external): Variable (depends on external arbitrator)
- Appeal window: 48 hours after each level

**Risk Management:**
- Freeze duration: 72 hours for first severe event, 7 days for repeat
- Unbond delay: 14 days (resolver), 21 days (senior)
- Epoch length: 7 days

## Migration Path

**v1 → v2**: Add appeal bonds and slashing incentives while keeping governance-selected resolvers.

**v2 → v3**: Enable decentralized resolver selection and capital-weighted delegation; increase bond requirements.

Each transition is **backward compatible**:
- v1 escrows remain v1 (no forced migration)
- v2 escrows remain v2
- New escrows choose their resolution mode at creation
