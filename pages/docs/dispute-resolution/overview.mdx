# What is Dispute Resolution?

Sew Protocol is built on the principle that protected transfers are **agreements between parties**. When both parties agree to the terms, everything works smoothly. But what happens when they disagree?

## The Problem

Imagine a protected transfer where:
- Sender deposits funds into escrow
- Recipient is supposed to deliver goods or services
- Recipient claims delivery was complete
- Sender disagrees

Without dispute resolution, the funds are **stuck indefinitely**. Neither party can act unilaterally. Dispute resolution exists to break this deadlock fairly.

## Core Design Principles

Sew's dispute resolution is built on three pillars:

### 1. **Deterministic Outcomes**

Disputes don't rely on arbitrary judgment. Instead, they follow predefined rules:
- Decisions must be justified by evidence
- Escalation paths are automatic
- Rules are the same for everyone

This prevents favoritism and makes the system predictable.

### 2. **Economic Incentives**

Participants are motivated to:
- **Resolve honestly**: Slashing penalties for wrong decisions
- **Avoid frivolous appeals**: Bond forfeiture for failed escalations
- **Operate reliably**: Better operators earn more workload

Incentives are baked into the protocol, not enforced by reputation alone.

### 3. **Escalation Safeguards**

Instead of a single point of failure, disputes can escalate:
- **Level 1**: Initial resolver makes a decision
- **Level 2**: Senior resolver can review
- **Level 3**: External arbitration (e.g., Kleros) for final binding outcome

Multiple levels prevent one bad actor from permanently mishandling disputes.

## Dispute Lifecycle

### 1. **Initiation**
Either party opens a dispute by submitting evidence and a claim. The dispute pauses normal release logic.

### 2. **Initial Review (Resolver)**
A resolver examines evidence and makes a decision:
- **Outcome A**: Full release to recipient
- **Outcome B**: Full refund to sender
- **Outcome C**: Split between parties

### 3. **Appeal (Optional)**
If either party disagrees, they can post an appeal bond and escalate to the next level. The bond is:
- **Forfeited** if the appeal fails (discourages frivolous escalation)
- **Returned** if the appeal succeeds

### 4. **Final Outcome**
Once all appeals are exhausted or the bond is forfeited, the decision becomes final and funds are distributed.

## Key Concepts

### **Resolver**
A designated operator who reviews disputes and makes decisions. Resolvers:
- Post bonds as collateral
- Earn fees for their work
- Face slashing penalties for misconduct or timeouts
- Can be selected based on category expertise or dispute value

### **Appeal Bond**
A deposit posted by the appellant to escalate a decision. If the higher-level resolver agrees with the original decision, the bond is forfeited and goes into the insurance pool.

### **Slashing**
An automatic penalty applied to resolvers for:
- **Timeout**: Missing a deadline (e.g., not making a decision within 24 hours)
- **Reversal**: Having their decision overturned by a higher-level resolver
- **Fraud**: Egregious misbehavior (e.g., requesting collateral from parties)

Slashing amounts are conservative by default (2%-10%) and can be adjusted via governance.

### **Freeze Logic**
If a resolver's bond falls below the minimum required, they are frozen:
- Cannot accept new disputes
- Cannot withdraw remaining stake
- Must replenish bond to resume operations

This prevents undercapitalized operators from accepting high-value disputes.

## Why Three Versions?

Sew's dispute resolution evolves in three phases:

### **v1: Single Trusted Resolver** (Launch)
- One governance-controlled resolver
- Fast and simple
- Suitable for bootstrap phase

### **v2: Decentralized Resolution** (Built, deferred until post-v1)
- Multiple resolvers with appeal bonds
- Resolver staking and slashing
- Economic security without decentralized selection

### **v3: Decentralized Incentives** (Future)
- Fully decentralized resolver selection
- Capital-weighted delegation
- Senior operators curate and train juniors
- Full capital-at-risk model

The evolution follows the principle: **"Decentralise decisions first, decentralise incentives second, decentralise capital last."**

Capital at risk creates adversarial pressure, so we only introduce it after decisions and incentives are proven stable.
