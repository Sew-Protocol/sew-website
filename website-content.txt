Emergency Recovery Sew Protocol This page documents the protocol's emergency response capabilities and recovery procedures. Emergency Philosophy Sew Protocol is designed with a containment-first approach to emergencies. The goal is to limit damage and preserve user funds, not to provide comprehensive recovery for all failure scenarios. Emergency mechanisms are intentionally constrained. They can reduce risk but cannot extract funds, modify existing escrows, or override settlement outcomes. Guardian Pause The guardian role can pause the protocol in response to critical vulnerabilities. Pause is an emergency circuit-breaker designed to prevent mass exploitation while fixes are prepared. What pause affects: New escrow creation Dispute initiation Dispute escalation What remains available during pause: Fund release (for non-disputed escrows) Fund cancellation (for non-disputed escrows) Existing position unwinding (where supported) Guardian Constraints Guardian powers are bounded to prevent abuse: Maximum pause duration 7 days Maximum pause cycles (per 90-day window) 3 pauses Can re-pause while paused No Can unpause Can extract funds No Can modify existing escrows No After the maximum duration, the pause automatically expires. Governance must manually unpause the system after addressing the underlying issue. Module Disabling The guardian can disable specific modules (such as yield generation integrations) without pausing the entire protocol. When used: A vulnerability is discovered in a specific integration An external protocol (e.g., Aave) experiences issues A resolution module shows problematic behavior Effects of module disable: New escrows cannot use the disabled module Existing escrows using the module may be restricted Other protocol functions continue normally Position Unwinding When an integration fails or poses risk, the protocol supports unwinding positions back to escrow custody. This allows users to exit risky positions while preserving their underlying funds. Unwinding process: Guardian identifies affected positions Unwind function is called for affected escrows Funds return to escrow custody Users can then release or cancel normally Unwinding preserves the principal. Yield may be forfeited depending on the timing and nature of the emergency. Emergency Response Timeline Phase Action Responsible Detection Issue identified (internal monitoring, external report, or incident disclosure) Team, auditors, or community Assessment Evaluate severity and scope of vulnerability Security team Containment Guardian pauses protocol or disables affected module Guardian Fix Preparation Develop and test remediation Development team Governance Review Timelock queue for fix deployment (if applicable) Governance Recovery Deploy fix, unwind positions if needed, unpause system What Emergency Mechanisms Cannot Do It is important to understand the boundaries of emergency response: Cannot recover funds sent to wrong addresses: Standard transaction errors are irreversible Cannot reverse settlement outcomes: Released, refunded, or resolved escrows are final Cannot modify escrow rules: Per-escrow immutability is absolute Cannot bypass release conditions: Settlement paths are deterministic Cannot compensate for external failures: Issues in connected protocols (e.g., oracle failures) may not be recoverable Post-Incident Process After an emergency is resolved: Incident report is published documenting what happened Root cause analysis is shared with the community Lessons learned are incorporated into protocol improvements Any emergency actions taken are documented and justified Related Pages Protocol Limits: Detailed constraints on guardian and governance powers Governance: How protocol changes are coordinated Security Model: Overall security philosophy and properties How It Works: Understanding escrow lifecycle Last updated: February 2026

Faq
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Can governance seize my funds? No. Governance can only change parameters for future escrows. Once funds are in an escrow, the rules governing that escrow are fixed. Governance cannot modify, redirect, or seize funds from any active or completed escrow. What happens if I send funds to the wrong address? Ethereum transactions are irreversible. If you send funds to an incorrect address, those funds cannot be recovered by the protocol, governance, or any third party. Always verify addresses before confirming transactions. What happens during a protocol pause? When paused, new escrow creation and dispute escalation are blocked. However: existing escrows can still be released, cancelled, or resolved; funds in active escrows remain safe; and the pause automatically expires after 7 days maximum. Is dispute resolution decentralized at launch? No. At launch, the protocol uses a single trusted resolver model. Decentralized dispute resolution (multi-resolver escalation with appeal bonds and staking) will be activated through governance after testing and validation—estimated Q4 2026. What chains are supported? Sew Protocol is deployed on Base (Ethereum L2). Multi-L2 support is designed into the architecture and may be added in future updates. Is Sew a wallet or marketplace? No. Sew is infrastructure—a security layer for payments. Wallets, marketplaces, and applications build on top of Sew to provide protected transfers. Everyday Wallet is one example interface built on the protocol. Are my funds at risk if an integration (like Aave) fails? Yield generation is optional and protected by exposure caps. If an external protocol fails, exposure is limited to the capped amount. Core escrow funds remain separate and can be settled regardless of yield module status. Can escrow rules be changed after creation? No. Each escrow captures its complete configuration at creation—release strategy, resolution module, yield settings, and timeouts. These are snapshotted and cannot be changed by anyone, including governance. What fees does Sew charge? Escrow creation: 1% at launch (2% max). Yield protocol fee: 30% of generated yield. Appeal bond fee: 0% at launch (30% max, governance-activated later). See the Fees page for details. How long does dispute resolution take? Dispute duration is configurable per escrow with a maximum of 90 days. Simple disputes under single-resolver mode may resolve in hours. Complex disputes using escalating resolution may take longer depending on the escalation path.

Glossary
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Protected Transfer An ERC-20 token transfer routed through escrow with predefined release and resolution rules. Funds are held under deterministic conditions until settlement. Protected Payment A user-friendly term for a protected transfer. Refers to sending value where funds are held in escrow until conditions are met. Escrow A smart contract that holds tokens under predefined conditions until release, refund, or resolution. Once created, its rules and configuration are fixed. Escrow State The current lifecycle stage of an escrow: Pending (active, awaiting action), Disputed (under resolution), Released (funds sent to recipient), Refunded (funds returned to sender), or Resolved (resolver determined outcome). Settlement The final outcome of an escrow where funds are either released to the recipient or returned to the sender. Deterministic Execution Outcomes are determined strictly by predefined rules in smart contracts, not by discretionary decisions. Per-Escrow Snapshot The full configuration of an escrow recorded at creation (modules, parameters, fees, timeouts). This configuration cannot be changed afterward. Forward-Only Upgrades A design principle where protocol upgrades affect only new escrows. Existing escrows continue operating under their original rules. Module A pluggable smart contract that defines a specific behavior within an escrow. Modules are selected at creation and remain fixed for that escrow. Release Strategy A module that defines when and how funds can be released. Examples include sender-initiated release, time-based release, or rule-based conditions. Resolution Module A module that defines how disputes are opened, escalated, and decided. Yield Module An optional module that can deploy escrowed funds into external protocols to generate yield while funds remain locked. Composable A system design where applications can integrate the protocol and extend it with new modules without modifying the core escrow logic. Non-Custodial Funds are held by smart contracts and controlled by predefined rules. No intermediary can arbitrarily redirect, seize, or access funds. Guardian A limited emergency role that can pause the protocol temporarily, disable integrations, or reduce exposure caps. Cannot withdraw funds or alter existing escrows. Timelock A delay mechanism for governance changes. Actions are queued and executed only after a defined waiting period, allowing review. Exposure Caps Limits on how much value can be routed into external integrations (such as yield protocols). Designed to contain risk and reduce systemic exposure. Dispute Resolution The process used when parties disagree on whether funds should be released or refunded. Resolution may involve a resolver or multi-level escalation. Appeal Bond A deposit required to escalate or appeal a dispute outcome. The bond may be forfeited if the appeal fails, discouraging low-quality challenges. ERC-20 A standard for fungible tokens on Ethereum that enables consistent transfer and integration across applications. Transfer (ERC-20 Transfer) The movement of tokens from one address to another on Ethereum. Peer-to-Peer Transfer A direct transfer of tokens between users without a centralized intermediary. Yield Additional tokens earned by temporarily deploying assets into lending or liquidity protocols. Liquidity Pool A shared pool of assets used by external protocols (e.g., lending systems). Users receive proportional claims on deposited assets. Smart Contract Code deployed on Ethereum that automatically executes predefined logic when triggered by transactions. Protocol A shared on-chain system of smart contracts and rules that anyone can interact with. It is infrastructure, not a service provider. Smart Contract Architecture The structured system formed by multiple interacting contracts that define how escrows are created, executed, and resolved. Transaction A signed instruction submitted to Ethereum that changes blockchain state (e.g., creating an escrow or releasing funds). Wallet Software that allows users to hold keys, sign transactions, and interact with smart contracts.

Governance Sew Protocol Sew Protocol is designed to evolve over time while preserving the integrity of existing agreements. Governance is structured around a simple principle: Protocol coordination should never become custody. Governance philosophy Sew separates two responsibilities: Protocol evolution Operational safety Governance is designed to: Allow defensive action when needed Prevent discretionary interference in active escrows Ensure upgrades affect only future agreements The goal is long-term stability, not constant change. Core design constraint Once an escrow is created, its rules are fixed. This means: The release strategy is locked The resolution path is locked The yield module (if selected) is locked The settlement logic cannot be rewritten Governance cannot retroactively alter how an existing escrow resolves. This is a foundational property of the system. Roles and responsibilities Sew uses role-based governance with clearly bounded authority. Timelock governance The timelock is responsible for protocol evolution. Typical responsibilities: Registering new modules Updating system configuration Setting exposure caps Managing supported integrations Appointing or removing guardian roles Adjusting protocol parameters Changes are delayed through a timelock mechanism to allow visibility and review before activation. This provides transparency, predictability, and time to react to risky changes. Guardian role The guardian exists for emergency response. Scope is intentionally limited. Typical actions: Disable integrations (e.g., yield module) Lower risk parameters (such as caps) Pause certain system components Guardian constraints: Pause maximum duration: 7 days Maximum 3 pauses per 90-day rolling window Cannot re-pause while already paused Unpause requires governance, not guardian The guardian cannot withdraw funds, redirect assets, rewrite settlement outcomes, or change the rules of existing escrows. The role is defensive, not managerial. Escrow contracts Escrow contracts are registered as authorized participants in modules. They initiate yield deposits, trigger withdrawals, and interact with protocol modules on behalf of users. This allows governance and operational controls to be scoped at the integration boundary. Exposure caps can be applied per escrow contract, and integrations can be managed without affecting unrelated components. Governance lanes Different types of changes use different timelines and approval requirements: Lane Typical Use Cases Delay Fast lane Small parameter adjustments, token registration, minor configuration changes 48 hours Slow lane Module upgrades, high-risk parameter changes, new integrations Emergency lane 0 hours (immediate) This layered approach balances agility with safety. Critical changes require more review time. Change boundaries There are clear limits on what governance can and cannot do. Governance can: Approve new modules Set exposure limits Enable or disable integrations Adjust system parameters for future activity Register new tokens Appoint or remove guardian roles Governance cannot: Seize funds from escrows Redirect assets Force settlement outcomes Change the rules of an active escrow Modify past decisions These constraints are enforced by contract design, not policy. Module governance Modules are the primary mechanism for protocol evolution. Each module type has specific governance considerations: Resolution modules Govern how disputes are handled. Changes to resolution logic only affect new escrows. Existing disputes continue under the resolution module selected at escrow creation. Release strategies Define who can release funds and under what conditions. New release strategies can be introduced for future escrows without affecting existing agreements. Yield generation modules Manage how escrowed funds generate yield. Governance can add new yield integrations or disable existing ones. Module changes only affect new escrows. Exposure management Governance manages system-level risk through exposure controls: Per-token caps: Maximum exposure for each supported token Per-contract caps: Maximum deposits into specific escrow contracts Module-level limits: Exposure limits for each integration These controls are applied at deposit time and designed to limit systemic risk, prevent over-concentration, and maintain stability across integrations. They do not affect the ability to withdraw or unwind existing positions. Trust assumptions Users rely on governance to act within defined constraints. Key assumptions: Timelock delays provide transparency into upcoming changes Guardians act to reduce risk, not increase it Governance powers remain narrowly scoped Role appointments follow proper procedures The protocol is designed so that even in worst-case governance failures: funds remain under contract control, settlement paths remain deterministic, and existing agreements stay protected. Long-term direction Governance is expected to decentralize over time. Early phases may involve core team stewardship, carefully managed module rollouts, and conservative exposure policies. As the system matures, governance participation can broaden, module development can expand, and decision-making can become more distributed. The underlying constraint remains: existing escrows must remain predictable and protected. Protocol Limits Emergency Recovery Last updated: February 2026

How It Works

How It Works Sew Protocol Sew Protocol is a security layer for payments on Base (Ethereum L2). Instead of transferring funds directly, payments can be held in escrow until conditions are met. Glossary: A protected payment is an ERC-20 transfer routed through escrow. "Transfer" refers to the on-chain token movement; "payment" refers to the user intent. What Sew Is Not Sew is not a marketplace, a wallet, or a court. Sew is a security layer for transfers. It provides the infrastructure for protected payments while leaving user experience, dispute evaluation, and interface design to the applications built on top. The core idea A standard token transfer is immediate and final. A protected transfer is different: Funds are placed into a smart contract Release conditions are defined up front Settlement happens when those conditions are met The protocol does not decide outcomes. It enforces the rules defined at creation. Each escrow captures its configuration at creation and cannot be changed. Governance changes only affect future escrows, never existing ones. Lifecycle of a protected transfer 1) Creation A user initiates a payment through an interface (such as a wallet or marketplace). At creation time, the following are defined: Token and amount Sender and recipient Release strategy Dispute resolution path (if enabled) Optional yield generation (if enabled) Funds are transferred into an escrow contract and held under these rules. Once created, these rules do not change. 2) Active escrow state While active, the funds remain locked in escrow. During this period: The recipient cannot withdraw funds unilaterally The sender cannot reclaim funds unilaterally Settlement depends on the selected release path This creates a neutral holding phase where both parties can coordinate. 3) Optional yield generation If enabled at creation: Funds may generate yield through integrations (e.g., Aave V3) Yield can go to sender, recipient, or be split Yield settings are fixed at escrow creation This feature is optional. Yield settings are locked at escrow creation and cannot be changed. Yield disclaimer: Yield is optional. Principal remains governed by escrow logic. External protocol risk exists. Exposure caps and unwind mechanisms limit potential losses. Users authorize actions; relayers may submit transactions. Relayers cannot control funds or override escrow rules. 4) Release Settlement typically occurs through a release action. Examples include: Sender releases at any time after creating the escrow Time-based release triggers automatically after a set period A predefined condition is met When released: funds leave escrow, the recipient receives the payment, and any generated yield is included in settlement. If a dispute is opened, release is paused until resolution. 5) Dispute (if needed) If parties disagree, a dispute can be opened. Resolution modes: Single trusted resolver (available at launch): Escalating resolution (staged rollout): At launch, the protocol uses single trusted resolver mode. Escalating resolution will be activated through governance after testing and validation. 6) Final settlement Once released, cancelled, or resolved: The escrow closes Funds are transferred to the correct destination No further changes are possible The agreement is complete. Escrow states An escrow moves through defined states during its lifecycle: Pending: Created and funded. Funds are locked until conditions are met. Disputed: A participant opened a dispute. Resolution is in progress. Released: Conditions were met. Recipient received the funds. Refunded: The escrow was cancelled. Funds returned to sender. Resolved: A resolver determined the outcome. Funds distributed accordingly. Once an escrow reaches Released, Refunded, or Resolved, it is complete. These states are final. Module architecture Sew uses pluggable modules to define escrow behavior: Release strategy: Controls who can release funds and under what conditions. Resolution module: Handles disputes and determines outcomes. Yield module: Manages optional yield generation on escrowed funds. Modules are selected when an escrow is created and cannot be changed afterward. This ensures existing agreements remain predictable regardless of future protocol upgrades. Safety mechanisms To support system stability: Governance can limit exposure to external integrations Guardians can disable risky integrations Positions can be unwound back to escrow custody in emergencies These mechanisms are designed to contain risk, not to control funds. Protocol Limits Fees Fees What Makes Sew Different Sew introduces several original design concepts: Per-escrow configuration snapshot Forward-only evolution: Protocol upgrades and module changes only affect new escrows. Existing agreements remain governed by the rules that existed when they were created. Yield on escrowed payments: Funds held in escrow can generate yield through integrations like Aave V3. This yield accrues to the escrow and is included in final settlement. Resolver incentives: Dispute resolvers are incentivized through bonds and fees to act honestly. Escalation paths with final arbitration (e.g., Kleros) provide recourse for disputed outcomes. Who can do what? Each role in the protocol has specific, bounded powers: Sender: Creates escrow, funds it, can release anytime, can dispute Recipient: Can release funds (if allowed), can dispute, cannot cancel Resolver: Decides disputes within their module, gets bonded incentives Guardian: Can pause (7-day max), disable integrations, lower caps Governance: Can upgrade modules, change parameters, appoint guardians No role can seize funds or modify rules of existing escrows. Summary Sew Protocol adds structure to token transfers without introducing custody. It allows a payment to be: Held Coordinated Settled Under predefined, enforceable rules. This makes it possible to support safer exchanges between parties who do not already trust each other.

Security
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Security Model Sew Protocol Sew Protocol is designed as a containment layer for protected transfers on Ethereum. Rather than attempting to eliminate all risk, the protocol focuses on narrowing scope, isolating failures, and ensuring that funds always follow deterministic paths. Security philosophy Ethereum transactions are irreversible. Smart contracts introduce new capabilities, but also new forms of risk. Sew approaches security through three principles: Containment over prevention: Failures may occur. The protocol is designed to limit their impact. Determinism over discretion: Funds move according to predefined rules, not human judgment. Isolation over shared risk: Each escrow is treated as an independent agreement. Core security properties Non-custodial by design Funds are held by smart contracts, not by an operator or intermediary. Deterministic release paths Every escrow defines its release and resolution rules at creation. Settlement follows predefined state transitions. Per-escrow isolation Each protected transfer is independent. If one fails, others are unaffected. Forward-only evolution Protocol upgrades do not alter existing agreements. Historical agreements remain stable. Threat model Sew is designed to operate in an adversarial environment, addressing risks like: User error: Sending funds to the wrong address or premature release. Counterparty risk: Fraudulent participants or failure to deliver. Smart contract risk: Bugs in integrations or extension modules. Governance risk: Malicious or compromised privileged roles. Yield is optional. Principal remains governed by escrow logic. External protocol risk exists. Exposure caps and unwind mechanisms limit potential losses. Emergency response posture In the event of a vulnerability, the protocol is designed for rapid containment. A guardian role can pause the system for up to 7 days (maximum 3 cycles total). While paused, dispute initiation and escalation are blocked to prevent further risk exposure. Normal settlement operations (release, cancellation, new escrow creation) remain available. Pause is an emergency circuit-breaker for high-risk operations and automatically expires after the time limit. Governance constraints Governance can approve new modules and update global parameters for future escrows. However, governance cannot rewrite existing agreements. Each escrow is locked to the modules and configuration selected at creation time. This ensures that protocol evolution does not introduce retroactive changes to active transfers. Users have permanent visibility into the rules governing their funds. Protocol Limits Emergency Recovery Last updated: February 2026
