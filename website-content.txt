About
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

About Sew Protocol Non-custodial infrastructure for protected transfers on Ethereum. Sew Protocol makes it possible to route ERC-20 transfers through escrow with predefined release and dispute resolution paths, without requiring custody or centralized trust. We believe protection should be available at the moment of transfer, not added later through third-party custody or platform-specific workarounds. Sew provides the neutral ground needed for secure onchain coordination. Core Escrow Non-custodial transfer protection with defined release and resolution rules enforced by code. Modular Design Pluggable release strategies, dispute resolvers, and yield modules selected at creation. Neutrality Sew does not take custody. No party can redirect funds outside predefined settlement paths. Forward-Only Module snapshots ensure that upgrades never retroactively alter active agreements. Evolution Roadmap Sew is designed for progressive decentralization. Our current phases: Phase 1 (Launch) Core escrow with single-tier trusted resolution. Phase 2 (Planned) Multi-tier resolution with staking and public appeals. Phase 3 (Vision) Fully decentralized resolver networks and delegation. System Design Architecture System structure and formal security invariants The Sew Protocol architecture is centered on a non-custodial Vault and a registry of swappable, immutable logic modules. Technical focus: How It Works. Architectural Job The primary role of the Sew architecture is containment. By strictly isolating agreement state and snapshotted logic, the protocol ensures that failures remain localized and governance cannot retroactively alter finalized terms. The module system The protocol snapshots active module addresses at creation, ensuring that historical agreements are never retroactively affected by governance updates. Core Vault Holds funds and manages the global state machine. Enforces transitions between Pending, Disputed, and Settled. Module Registry The canonical set of approved logic implementations. Swappable by governance, but snapshotted by escrows. Pluggable Logic Namespaced implementations for Release, Resolution, and Yield. Logic is immutable and isolated per escrow. Security invariants Formal properties enforced at the contract layer to ensure predictable coordination. Non-custodial Funds held by contract. No operator or module can redirect assets outside defined transitions. Isolated Each escrow is independent. State is namespaced by (vault, workflowId) to prevent failure propagation. Forward-only Upgrades apply only to new escrows. Active agreements remain locked to their snapshotted modules. Balance Integrity Modules must return all funds or revert. Emergency Recovery Containment-first response with bounded guardian powers This page documents the protocol's emergency response capabilities and recovery procedures. Emergency Philosophy Sew Protocol is designed with a containment-first approach to emergencies. The goal is to limit damage and preserve user funds, not to provide comprehensive recovery for all failure scenarios. Emergency mechanisms are intentionally constrained. They can reduce risk but cannot extract funds, modify existing escrows, or override settlement outcomes. Guardian Pause The guardian role can pause the protocol in response to critical vulnerabilities. Pause is an emergency circuit-breaker designed to prevent mass exploitation while fixes are prepared. What pause affects: New escrow creation Dispute initiation Dispute escalation What remains available during pause: Fund release (for non-disputed escrows) Fund cancellation (for non-disputed escrows) Existing position unwinding (where supported) Guardian Constraints Guardian powers are bounded to prevent abuse: Maximum pause duration 7 days Maximum pause cycles (per 90-day window) 3 pauses Can re-pause while paused No Can unpause Can extract funds No Can modify existing escrows No After the maximum duration, the pause automatically expires. Governance must manually unpause the system after addressing the underlying issue. Module Disabling The guardian can disable specific modules (such as yield generation integrations) without pausing the entire protocol. When used: A vulnerability is discovered in a specific integration An external protocol (e.g., Aave) experiences issues A resolution module shows problematic behavior Effects of module disable: New escrows cannot use the disabled module Existing escrows using the module may be restricted Other protocol functions continue normally Position Unwinding When an integration fails or poses risk, the protocol supports unwinding positions back to escrow custody. This allows users to exit risky positions while preserving their underlying funds. Unwinding process: Guardian identifies affected positions Unwind function is called for affected escrows Funds return to escrow custody Users can then release or cancel normally Unwinding preserves the principal. Yield may be forfeited depending on the timing and nature of the emergency. Emergency Response Timeline Phase Action Responsible Detection Issue identified (internal monitoring, external report, or incident disclosure) Team, auditors, or community Assessment Evaluate severity and scope of vulnerability Security team Containment Guardian pauses protocol or disables affected module Guardian Fix Preparation Develop and test remediation Development team Governance Review Timelock queue for fix deployment (if applicable) Governance Recovery Deploy fix, unwind positions if needed, unpause system What Emergency Mechanisms Cannot Do It is important to understand the boundaries of emergency response: Cannot recover funds sent to wrong addresses: Standard transaction errors are irreversible Cannot reverse settlement outcomes: Released, refunded, or resolved escrows are final Cannot modify escrow rules: Per-escrow immutability is absolute Cannot bypass release conditions: Settlement paths are deterministic Cannot compensate for external failures: Issues in connected protocols (e.g., oracle failures) may not be recoverable Post-Incident Process After an emergency is resolved: Incident report is published documenting what happened Root cause analysis is shared with the community Lessons learned are incorporated into protocol improvements Any emergency actions taken are documented and justified Related Pages Protocol Limits: Detailed constraints on guardian and governance powers Governance: How protocol changes are coordinated Security Model: Overall security philosophy and properties How It Works: Understanding escrow lifecycle Last updated: February 2026

Faq
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Can governance seize my funds? No. Governance can only change parameters for future escrows. Once funds are in an escrow, the rules governing that escrow are fixed. Governance cannot modify, redirect, or seize funds from any active or completed escrow. What happens if I send funds to the wrong address? Ethereum transactions are irreversible. If you send funds to an incorrect address, those funds cannot be recovered by the protocol, governance, or any third party. Always verify addresses before confirming transactions. What happens during a protocol pause? When paused, new escrow creation and dispute escalation are blocked. However: existing escrows can still be released, cancelled, or resolved; funds in active escrows remain safe; and the pause automatically expires after 7 days maximum. Is dispute resolution decentralized at launch? No. At launch, the protocol uses a single trusted resolver model. Decentralized dispute resolution (multi-resolver escalation with appeal bonds and staking) will be activated through governance after testing and validation—estimated Q4 2026. What chains are supported? Sew Protocol is deployed on Base (Ethereum L2). Multi-L2 support is designed into the architecture and may be added in future updates. Is Sew a wallet or marketplace? No. Sew is infrastructure—a security layer for transfers. Wallets, marketplaces, and applications build on top of Sew to provide protected transfers. Everyday Wallet is one example interface built on the protocol. Are my funds at risk if an integration (like Aave) fails? Yield generation is optional and protected by exposure caps. If an external protocol fails, exposure is limited to the capped amount. Core escrow funds remain separate and can be settled regardless of yield module status. Can escrow rules be changed after creation? No. Each escrow captures its complete configuration at creation—release strategy, resolution module, yield settings, and timeouts. These are snapshotted and cannot be changed by anyone, including governance. What fees does Sew charge? Escrow creation: 1% at launch (2% max). Yield protocol fee: 30% of generated yield. Appeal bond fee: 0% at launch (30% max, governance-activated later). See the Fees page for details. How long does dispute resolution take? Dispute duration is configurable per escrow with a maximum of 90 days. Simple disputes under single-resolver mode may resolve in hours. Complex disputes using escalating resolution may take longer depending on the escalation path.

Glossary
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Appeal Bond A deposit required to escalate or appeal a dispute outcome. The bond may be forfeited if the appeal fails, discouraging low-quality challenges. Composable A system design where applications can integrate the protocol and extend it with new modules without modifying the core escrow logic. customResolver A per-escrow field that overrides the protocol default resolver with a specific address. Allows arbitrary dispute frameworks to be used without modifying protocol code. Deterministic Execution Outcomes are determined strictly by predefined rules in smart contracts, not by discretionary decisions. Dispute Architecture The structural layer of dispute handling: state machines, resolver interfaces, escalation paths, and onchain enforcement. Dispute Economics The incentive layer of dispute handling: bond sizing, slashing rates, and resolver accountability. Dispute Resolution The process used when parties disagree on whether funds should be released or refunded. May involve a single resolver or multi-level escalation. ERC-20 The standard for fungible tokens on Ethereum, used by Sew for all protected transfers. Escrow A smart contract that holds tokens under predefined conditions until release, refund, or resolution. Escrow State The current lifecycle stage of an escrow: PENDING (active), DISPUTED (under resolution), RELEASED (finalized to recipient), REFUNDED (returned to sender), or RESOLVED (determined by resolver). Exposure Caps Governance-enforced limits on how much value can be routed into specific external modules or protocols. Forward-Only Upgrades A design principle where protocol upgrades affect only new escrows. Existing agreements remain bound to their snapshotted modules. Guardian A limited emergency role that can pause high-risk operations temporarily. Cannot redirect funds or alter existing agreements. Invariant A formal property enforced at the contract level that holds true for every escrow, such as non-custodial or per-escrow isolation. Module A pluggable, immutable smart contract that defines a specific logic behavior (Release, Resolution, or Yield) within an escrow. Non-Custodial A model where funds are held by smart contracts rather than intermediaries. No operator can arbitrarily redirect assets. Per-Escrow Snapshot The permanent recording of active module addresses at the moment of escrow creation, ensuring immunity from retroactive changes. Protected Transfer An ERC-20 transfer routed through a deterministic escrow with predefined release and dispute resolution paths. Release Strategy A module defining when and how funds can be settled, such as sender-initiated, time-based, or conditional release. Resolution Module A module defining how disputes are opened, escalated, and decided by resolvers. Resolver A smart contract or entity that determines the outcome of a dispute. Resolvers are bounded to protocol-defined outcomes (Release or Refund). Settlement The final distribution of escrowed funds to either the recipient (Release) or the sender (Refund). State Machine The set of valid states and irreversible transitions an escrow moves through from creation to settlement. Timelock A governance delay mechanism that ensures all protocol changes are queued for review before execution. workflowId A unique identifier assigned to each protected transfer, used for querying state and interacting with modules. Yield Module An optional module that manages principal deployment into external protocols (like Aave V3) to generate yield while funds are locked.

Security
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Security First Security Model Primary reference for auditors and security teams Sew Protocol is designed as a containment layer for protected transfers on Ethereum. We focus on narrowing scope, isolating failures, and ensuring funds always follow deterministic paths. Jump to: Threat Model Dispute Economics Upgrade Semantics Emergency Recovery Isolation Model Security philosophy Ethereum transactions are irreversible. Sew approaches security through three core principles: Containment Failures may occur. The protocol is designed to limit their impact to the affected escrow. Determinism Funds move according to predefined rules, not discretionary human judgment. Isolation Each escrow is treated as an independent agreement with no shared state risk. Core security properties Funds are held by smart contracts, not by an operator or intermediary. Every escrow defines its rules at creation. Settlement follows predefined state transitions. Each protected transfer is independent. If one fails, others are unaffected. Upgrades do not alter existing agreements. Historical agreements remain stable. Threat model Sew is designed to operate in an adversarial environment. Each threat category includes the attack surface and structural mitigation. T1 Risk: Sender enters incorrect recipient or misunderstands the active release conditions. Escrow terms are visible before confirmation; pending escrows can follow predefined cancellation or dispute paths. The protocol cannot recover funds sent via ordinary direct transfers outside the Sew ecosystem. T2 Risk: Recipient fails to deliver goods or services after escrow is funded. Funds remain in escrow until conditions are met. Either party can initiate a dispute to trigger a resolver review. The protocol is an enforcement layer, not a fact-finding oracle. Final decisions rely on the assigned resolver. T3 Risk: A bug in a specific yield module or integration leads to loss of principal. Per-escrow isolation and strictly namespaced module state limit the blast radius. Snapshots prevent retroactive bugs. The protocol cannot reverse losses originating in external integration platforms (e.g. Aave V3). T4 Risk: Malicious governance proposal attempts to alter agreement outcomes or seize funds. Upgrades are forward-only. Governance cannot touch active escrows or modify snapshotted module addresses. Isolation model Isolation is the primary containment mechanism, operating at three levels simultaneously. Between escrows Independent agreements. No shared funds or shared dispute state. Failure in one cannot propagate. Between modules Release, resolution, and yield logic are isolated. A bug in yield cannot affect resolution. Economic incentives Resolvers are incentivized to behave honestly through economic mechanisms: bonds, appeals, and slashing. Resolver bonds Resolvers post collateral when deciding. If overturned on appeal, they lose the bond. Appeal escalation Disputed decisions escalate to higher-tier resolvers with larger bonds and senior review. Governance and upgrades Governance can approve new logic modules and adjust protocol-wide caps, but cannot rewrite existing agreements or redirect funds held in active escrows. Emergency containment A Guardian role can pause high-risk operations (such as dispute initiation) for up to 7 days during an active exploit. Normal settlement operations remain available to users.
